Top 10 Books of 2022

After the failures of the previous year's reading, your correspondent decided to go back to the basics: those novels which are immediately delightful, humorous, and moving. Keeping close to home proved a wise decision; it reminded him that for all the satisfaction of challenging, thoughtful works, sometimes all one really needs are those books and writers closest to one's heart. Here are your correspondent's top 10 books of 2022.

10. To Paradise by Hanya Yanagihara.

Your correspondent was pleased with the attention Hanya Yanagihara—the author of A Little Life[1], one of his favorite books—received with the publication of To Paradise: a profile in The New Yorker, stacks of signed hardcovers in every bookstore visited, banner ads and at least one subway poster. He was less pleased with the book itself: written in her same vivid, tactile prose—he's never loved the word "buttery" so much—but misshapen, uneven, and unsatisfying as a novel. The volume made the list at all almost exclusively on the merit of its sheer writing quality. It has a navel-gazing sensibility that begins as cleverly abrasive and finishes as abrasive; your correspondent cannot in all honesty endorse the book.

9. World Order by Henry Kissinger.

Henry Kissinger weaves a compelling, well-argued narrative of the two arms which balance world order: power and legitimacy. Legitimacy without power is useless; power without legitimacy is a death sentence. The sources of legitimacy, and their impact on the gain, wielding, and loss of power were Kissinger's biggest contributions; no book, let alone one coming in at just about 350 pages, can explain perfectly all the cogs behind the rise and fall of empires—but Kissinger's comes close.

8. The Twenty-Seventh City by Jonathan Franzen.

Jonathan Franzen's debut novel was well-received critically but sold poorly, and it's not a big wonder why: it's moralistic, unhumorous (or rather anti-humorous), serious, a little unapproachable—an uptight Midwesterner of a book. But it also has, fully-formed, the best qualities of Franzen's writing: sympathetic, human characters; perfectly tuned dialogue, in hostility and in seduction; descriptions of everyday occurrences and objects that come to mind the next time you yourself encounter them. Its legacy may be as the first book of a writer better known for his later works, but it is still an admirable work in its own right.

7. [Human Acts] by Han Kang.

Han's most tragic novel by far, Human Acts ("The Boy Comes" in Korean, the language in which your correspondent read the book) concerns the Gwangju Demonstration of 1980, whose casualties include three thousand massacred and thousands more detained, tortured, raped, and otherwise abused by government troops and authorities. The vileness of the murders, the trauma of survival, the pain to bear witness—Human Acts captures and stares down all of it in lucid, cold prose.

6. The Book of Salt by Monique Truong.

Contemporary American fiction's maximalism—DeLillo's Underworld and Wallace's Infinite Jest, for two canonical examples—is distilled into under 300 pages, spiced with the immigrant experience, and garnished with Gertrude Stein and Alice Toklas in Monique Truong's fantastic debut novel. Its prose is as lush, rich, and suffocating as your correspondent imagines the Vietnamese humidity to be; there is something to its sheer density that precludes extended reading, which comes off as a weakness. But that same density is crucial to the lushness and richness that makes the work so good: the extended food metaphors, the domestic life of Stein and Toklas, the tragedy beflecking every character in the book. It is a work felt primarily in the gut—and that is intended most highly.

5. Brief Interviews with Hideous Men by David Foster Wallace.

David Foster Wallace's three novels are each so sui generis that someone who loves one may well brush aside another. His short stories offer a greater consistency as to their quality: absolutely fantastic. The "interviews" themselves carry on Infinite Jest's narrative style—questions replaced by "Q"—and overall atmosphere—darkly humorous and humorously dark. The other short stories inspire laughter, groaning, and more than a few sighs and "Oh my God"s; in particular, "Adult World I" and "Adult World II," "Forever Overhead," and "Church Not Made With Hands" are dyed with the same wit, gentleness, and subtlety that made your correspondent fall in love with Wallace in the first place. He may break the upcoming year's resolution not to buy new books to tackle the first two books by this astounding writer.

4. On Such a Full Sea by Chang-Rae Lee.

Your correspondent's partner bought this book for him after reading it for class (as with the immediately preceding entry), and though he found the start of the novel challenging to get into, it ended up ranking quite high on this year's list. Why? Answering this question, he must begin with the narration: omniscient first-person plural—an unidentified group watching the protagonist along her journey to find her beloved, taken away for unknown experiments after being found to be immune to "C." The tone is admiring, elegiac, tender; your correspondent's partner compared it to the chorus in a Greek tragedy. And tragic it is: one mourns the world it draws and the people who inhabit its barren, scarred lands. And amidst its apocalyptic setting, the book also captures better than any other book the duty of filial piety felt by Asian children. Probably the most touching books of the year.

3. Blindness by Jose Saramago.

The prize motivation of Jose Saramago's Nobel Prize declared that he, "with parables sustained by imagination, compassion, and irony, continually enables us once again to apprehend an elusory reality." This rings true of Blindness, a far darker, more haunting Lord of the Flies. The prose is direct, simple (almost) to a fault; the observations are neutral, wry, at times expressed with a suppressed chuckle. Its true magnificence comes from its ability to conjure beautiful moments and believe in the best of people despite the world replete with evil and death within its pages. About this book more than any other book on this list, your correspondent extols its human incandescence and discourages the faint of heart.

2. The Thousand Autumns of Jacob de Zoet by David Mitchell.

Your correspondent adores anything David Mitchell writes, so it's curious that he reads him so infrequently—Cloud Atlas twice and Slade House once, both in high school. This brilliant novel set in Dejima—a tiny port which was the only area of Japan open to Europeans for 300 years—bursts with the most eclectic characters: the titular clerk, but also a daring midwife, a corrupt port administrator (redundant?), a brash American general (ditto), and a deist polymath who uses an execution by guillotine as an opportunity for an anatomy lesson. These characters' sometimes-farcical misadventures contrast with horrors beyond imagining on the mainland; add Mitchell's ear and creativity for the most inventive dialogue of any writer alive today, and one gets nothing short of an underrated masterpiece.

1. The Goldfinch by Donna Tartt.

Your correspondent cannot think of any three-book oeuvre praised and idolized as highly as that of Donna Tartt. Her debut novel was edged out in your correspondent's list for 2020 only by virtue of overwhelming competition; her most recent novel is hilarious, moving, and saddening in an autumnal sort of way, often at the same time. (See the fourteen-year-old protagonist saying farewell to his only friend, the son of a mining tycoon, with a duffel and a dog in tow: "I stopped myself from blurting the thing on the edge of my tongue, the thing I'd never said, even though it was something we both knew well enough … which was, of course, I love you.") One other thing worth pointing out: as with Wallace and tennis or Melville and whaling, Tartt has the remarkable ability to write such that one can read pages upon pages about a niche topic, in this case furniture and restoration. Your correspondent gulped down this nearly 800-page novel in perhaps two weeks; he cannot recommend it highly enough.

Bonus <> a book not worth reading: Lake Success by Gary Shteyngart.

One of your correspondent's favorite short stories to run in The New Yorker is "The Luck of Kokura," an excerpt of this novel. After all, what's not to like about a moronic financier embarrassing himself while hanging out with a former employee whom he fired after a stray negative sign lost their company a hundred million dollars? A great deal, it turns out, when it goes on for more than a dozen pages. A plot as sagging and hapless as the protagonist—its focal point is a road trip "to experience the real America," somehow even worse for being ironic than if it had been earnest—and characters as ridiculous as the obscene wealth they possess make for a feeling not unlike pity for the author. Turns out that "The Luck of Kokura" was better left as a short story.

1. Despite being read this year, A Little Life was omitted from this year's list for having been included in 2019's list.
