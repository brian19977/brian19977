Theater Review: Merry Wives by William Shakespeare at Shakespeare in the Park

The Summer of Our Content: Merry Wives in Central Park

The Public Theater's "Shakespeare in the Park" staged Merry Wives—adapted from The Merry Wives of Windsor—for its return from a two-year hiatus. Since its last season, America has seen an impeachment, a pandemic, the Black Lives Matter protests, and the most contentious presidential election in modern history. It fell to adapter Jocelyn Bioh and director Saheem Ali to respond to this moment with theater that excited deprived show-goers, reunified a polarized citizenry, and welcomed people of all backgrounds. Their answer: a "raucous comedy" which, after the "year and a half that everyone had been through," emphasized the "joy and vitality" of everyday life. Grounding that vision are the values of openness and accessibility—openness to actors of color, and accessibility to all attendees, especially people like Bioh's "Ghanaian aunties and uncles," to whom Shakespeare can be "alienating." But in aspiring to cultural ecumenism, the play too frequently leaned on empty gestures and rhetoric.

The casting and acting usually succeeded in conveying the joyful and vibrant lives of South Harlem, but occasionally felt uneven or superficial. The all-black cast was beflecked with diverse accents and clothes; African-Americans, like the buffoonish Falstaff, and immigrants from Nigeria, Ghana, Senegal, and elsewhere live beside one another. Falstaff's untied sneakers and rumpled T-shirts contrast with Madam Ford's vibrant Nigerian dresses and Madam Page's flowing Ghanaian pants, heightening the preposterousness of his scheme to trick them into falling for him. Yet the portrayal of other characters felt clumsy, particularly the well-off, Senegalese Dr. Caius. In the original, he is French—the subject of jokes and exclusion. Now, the parody is milder: his accent and dress aren't caricatured. But the French phrases persist, as do overwrought gestures like cheek- and hand-kisses. Stereotypes endure—they're just now directed to the wealthy and Francophone. And Fenton's casting as a woman and Dr. Caius's delight at his male match pointed to a break with Shakespeare's heteronormative mold, but felt shallow at best and discomfiting at worst: no one minds that one marries gay—only that one doesn't marry poor.

The location and set represented some of the play's greatest strengths: it showcased the vitality of Harlem life and expanded how theatergoers might conceive Shakespeare, but sometimes relied on gags to achieve their effect. The entire play takes place on a Harlem street featuring a braiding salon, laundromat, and walk-in clinic—commonplace establishments which Harlemites would be pleased to see honored onstage. (Though "Windsor" was excised from the title, a building on-set bears its name—a flourish typical of Harlem residences.) But one could do without the CitiBike and manhole. Both were used just once, and a bike trundling across-stage and a person popping from an underground hole have the virtue of amusing children and little else.

By its end, this show, with its many changes—some Covid-induced, others deliberate—delivers on its promised accessibility, but with perhaps too much hollow theatricality. Before the play, a drummer leads the audience in a call-and-response in unidentified African languages. Generously speaking, it's a crowd-exciting moment, but frankly evokes superficial "cultural immersions" with souvenirs of masks or leis. And halfway through, Falstaff breaks character to talk about his "boredom" and "Netflix bingeing" during the pandemic—a privileged and meaningless prattle that adds little to the play while baldly playing to the audience. Even if the Latin lessons and the "master" and "mistress" are cut, Merry Wives does both too little and too much to be the broadly welcoming act it wants to be. It's joyful and appealing, sure—but not much of anything else.
