Concert Review: Paul Lewis, 16 May 2019

Certain names carry with them an immediate air of importance, or at least provocation: hear “Garrick Ohlsson” or “Wilhelm Korstick” and one is inclined to turn the head. “Paul Lewis” is not such a name; Mr. Lewis very well could be a carpenter, an accountant, or an airplane pilot. He is none of the three, at least not professionally: rather, he is a world-class interpreter of Beethoven and Schubert, already having completed a cycle of Beethoven sonatas and recorded most of Schubert’s late solo keyboard works. This evening, he graced the intimate Kaufmann Hall of the 92nd St. Y with a sonata by Haydn; the Three Intermezzi, opus 117, by Brahms; and Beethoven’s colossal Diabelli Variations. Though the program is one of your correspondent's favorites this season, oversights—interpretive, technical, and performative—led to an ultimately lackluster and even displeasing evening.

The opening sonata was finely performed: rapid arpeggiated sequences were deftly executed; dramatic pauses were liberally extended; long melodic lines were sustained to evoke a singing tone. The only true criticism of the piece that your correspondent can think of, in fact, is that it is rather too saccharine: Haydn was writing in an era where much of the keyboard music, solo or chamber, was to be performed in a domestic or at least private setting. Thus, the severity of his most famous pupil’s music, such as that found in the Appassionata Sonata or the Eroica Variations, is replaced with rather genteel melodies, predictable modulations, repetitive (if not outright wearisome) passagework and transitions, and prim cadences. To place beside this piece the rich, haunting Intermezzi and the witty Diabelli Variations is almost to make a mockery of Haydn. And yet, your correspondent greatly enjoyed the performance; Mr. Lewis, too, seemed to relax somewhat, bowing with relaxed shoulders—a pleasant change after his tense, hunched entry.

It was during the Intermezzi that your correspondent began to wonder: who is snoring? Alas, it was Mr. Lewis’s rattling, mildly phlegmy inhalations. One cannot place much blame at all for those particular auditory distractions, but, soon, others began to stand out also: the clicking of the left heel along to the beat, as though he were a jazz ensemble’s drummer; the harsh stomping on the sustaining pedal, especially in moments of excitement; and excited, heavy breathing during dense, technical passages. It is written in the program that Mr. Lewis was taken on as a private pupil by the piano titan Alfred Brendel; if not the music teacher, the conservatory instructor, the reviewer, or the audio engineer, Mr. Brendel ought to have excised these incongruous habits from him. Mr. Lewis does not by any means thrive in the persona of an eccentric or a hermit; he should resist the urges or impulses to act in the manner of one.

But, returning to the music: despite the disturbances, these were some of the most lucid interpretations of late Brahms by any performer—certainly fit to stand beside Mr. Brendel. The first intermezzo, in E-flat Major, is a richly layered, contrapuntal masterpiece—a tone poem in miniature (a compliment which your correspondent can heartily pay to all three of the Intermezzi). Brahms’s talent, frankly speaking, lay not in his careful craft of melodies, but in constructing vast edifices from prosaic harmonies and motives. From the simplest theme—an excerpt from the tonic scale, undergirded by the tonic octave—Brahms conjures, practically spools out of thin air, the most touching, lamenting lullaby.

Your correspondent must confess that this imagery is originally Brahms’s; he prefaces this intermezzo with these lines from a Scottish ballad: “Balow, my babe, lie still and sleep! / It grieves me sore to see thee weep.” The baby, however, seems nowhere to be seen: the poignancy of this piece more closely resembles a mother long after her worst nightmare, breathing in the scent of a blanket tinged with memories of her passed baby.

The second intermezzo was no less excellent: smoothly delineated arpeggios flowed beneath a theme deceptively simple, but, observed in a particular lens—a different dynamic, say, or an unusual articulation—that same theme seemed to transfigure itself from moody to jovial to despairing. B-flat Minor is a complex, dark key—miles apart from any other except perhaps its even more brooding cousin, E-flat Minor—and both Brahms and Mr. Lewis took full advantage of the musical opportunities presented.

The final intermezzo, in C-sharp Minor, contrasts between its two themes: the opening, dark and menacing; and the music-box-esque, slightly mysterious, even eerie second theme. Hints of the earlier Paganini Variations could be heard in the latter theme; altogether, these three were a welcome change from the Haydn sonata and an affirmation of Mr. Lewis’s musical sensitivity.

Following the intermission, Mr. Lewis dove into Beethoven’s Diabelli Variations. The waltz upon which it is based is a patriotic anthem by one Anton Diabelli, who asked for variations from his contemporary composers. Schubert, Liszt, Czerny, and Schumann all contributed one; Beethoven, however, declined to do so, but instead published his—all thirty-three of them—separately, in a work he titled, as noted in the manuscript, Veranderungen—more “transformations” than “variations.” (The first edition is titled the more widely used Variationen.) If one is perfectly frank, the first half needn’t have been there at all—Dame Uchida didn’t think so (the Diabelli Variations comprise the entirety of her upcoming recital at Carnegie Hall)—and so mastery over this set drives directly to the heart of the evening. But Mr. Lewis’s performance was wayward, disorganized, and lacking in gravitas—such that one might wonder how he has garnered so much respect from his recordings.

The tema (that is, the waltz by Diabelli vs. a theme, which every variation has), marked allegro, must have a bouncy, jaunty feel—imagine the spring in the steps of children at play. The off-beat emphases and syncopated bass motifs create a mood of jest; when played too fast, or with excessive pedal, the spark of energy is lost. This evening, rather than the merry-go-round whirring cheerily, it was malfunctioning—spinning to fast, it seems about to careen off its axis. Mr. Lewis was unable to rein in the excitement he seemed intent on expressing; rather than tethering the music to a consistent beat, he gratuitously accelerated and decelerated, stamping both feet all the while.

The next few variations proved to be an improvement on the theme: the first, alla Marcia, was grand and stately without being overbearing; in the fourth, the rising scalar theme, which riffs on a tiny transitional motif from the tema, bounced from left hand to right and bass register to treble in a quirky and delightful manner. But in the sixth, Mr. Lewis once again lost control of the music, as his overeager trills failed to keep in time with the beat, resulting in a haphazard and unstable performance. Not to mention that the variation began ff, when the previous one ended pp: Beethoven enjoyed writing rhetorical flourishes or dramatic gestures into music, but one wonders whether he would have enjoyed the jump-scare that Mr. Lewis gave his audience.

Throughout much of the remainder of the performance, these interpretive—well, piques, to be kind, and excesses, to be blunt, resulted in a word that keeps returning to the fore of your correspondent’s mind: incongruous. Taken together, these variations should add up to something greater than the sum of their parts. But relatively minor details—that Mr. Lewis seemed not to care about the distinction between mp, p, and pp; or that one variation’s allegro became another’s presto; or that, for all his foot-tapping, he never could seem to keep a consistent tempo within a single variation—made the listening experience an arduous one.

This even treaded the line into painful once, when Mr. Lewis thought it fitting or funny to treat his audience to another jump-scare: this time, most unmusically slamming base chords while trilling as hard as he could, at the start of the twenty-first variation, after the twentieth ended p. One woman in the row in front of your correspondent covered her ears in protest—granted, she was in the fourth row, but that should never be the case in any performance of Beethoven. Such egregious treatment of people who wanted an enjoyable evening, or, in the case of your correspondent, a top-notch performance of one of his favorite pieces, borders on rude. Mr. Brendel touted the Diabelli Variations as a prime example of how classical music need not be serious; indeed, the thirteenth variation, with its call-and-response style, and the twentieth, with its quotations from Leporello’s opening aria in Don Giovanni, were quite humorous. But whereas the performer might indulge in some fun—a hand gesture here or a smile there—never should focus be lost; Mr. Lewis’s humor strays far closer to irreverent than to tasteful.

Mr. Lewis almost redeemed himself in the final five variations—pensive, emotionally charged, superbly difficult soundscapes that demand all of a performer’s interpretive prowess after forty-five minutes of already-difficult music. Some incongruities peeked their heads again—rests were ignored in the twenty-ninth variation but emphasized in the thirty-first; the andante of the thirtieth was at least fifty percent faster than the immediately preceding adagio. Nevertheless, the voicing of the contrapuntal lines in the thirtieth were supremely compelling, and the chromatic flourishes in the thirty-first were excellently, and seemingly effortlessly, managed. The tempo of the thirty-second variation, though, was at least as fast as some of the presto variations that came before; the variation, a marvelous triple fugue whose primary theme is simply ten repeated notes, had a somewhat rushed atmosphere throughout, despite Mr. Lewis’s technical prowess. And the minuet of the final, thirty-third variation was executed precisely, if somewhat unimpressively: an ethereal haze of sound can almost be felt in the most well-done interpretations, but Mr. Lewis treated this music no differently than the preceding variations, and so no eerie transcendence was had.

The final chord of the Diabelli Variations is marked f, a point of some dispute among pianists. Most play it as marked—Richter, Ugorski, and Pollini all do so—but, in your correspondent’s view, the more sensible minority, Anderszewski fore among them, plays it p, hardly louder than the immediately preceding pp. This single note encapsulates the crux of your correspondent’s issue with Mr. Lewis. While he has no qualms about deviating from the written score—a position your correspondent wholeheartedly supports, since he believes that music is written to be enjoyed, not microscopically examined and worshiped—the deviations were inconsistently done or did not result in a better musical performance. Thus, when the concert ended, your correspondent felt no need to join the standing ovation nor to hear the encore, Beethoven’s bagatelle op. 126, no. 5. He finds it unlikely that he will return to a concert Mr. Lewis hosts—a pity, given that he cherishes his CDs of Schubert’s Wanderer Fantasy and middle-period Beethoven. Never meet your heroes.
