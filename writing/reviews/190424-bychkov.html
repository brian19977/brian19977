Concert Review: Semyon Bychkov with the New York Philharmonic, 24 April 2019

In late April, an entire country seems afflicted by that curious malaise of pre-final exams blues. Students fall asleep in or skip class; teachers return essays a month after submission, stained with coffee, or both; dormitory administrators expect to fit sixteen hundred freshmen into four hundred rooms. The natural relief from such incomprehensibility is jadedness and a half-smirk; but, for your correspondent, the salve of choice for his bludgeoned and drained spirit was a night at the philharmonic, where Mr. Semyon Bychkov conducted a US premiere and Brahms's Fourth Symphony.

The first piece was Thomas Larcher's Symphony No. 2, Kenotaph. The music was Mr. Larcher's reaction to the refugee crisis: a "cry of despair," he said in a pre-concert talk, in response to the tens of thousands who have died and will continue to die while crossing the Mediterranean on their way to Europe. Mr. Larcher said that this symphony was the product of his simultaneous horror and hopelessness at the crisis.

The symphony opened with a crash coupled with tense passages of strings; violins that sounded as though they were being played with sandpaper and trombones which were likely muted with plungers surfaced from the soup of sound here and there. Your correspondent gleaned ghosts of a Sonata-Allegro structure and wisps of recurrent motifs; however, after a prolonged first movement, your correspondent settled his jaw in his hand and woke up to applaud before intermission.

The gem of the night was Brahms's Fourth Symphony. Perhaps your correspondent would have a better idea of how the two symphonies of the evening were related had consciousness not departed him early on in the evening, but the two pieces were worlds apart, in the ideas they sought to convey, the images to conjure, and the mood to evoke, not to mention the eras into which they were born. It seems demeaning to compare Brahms's unrequited love for Clara Schumann to the unrequited hopes of refugees seeking a better life, and yet that is the first connection that your correspondent sees between the two works.

Whatever the etiology of these two works, one of their similarities is an incredible attention to detail on the part of both composers. Mr. Larcher scored his symphony for over 100 different instruments, while Brahms toiled over his symphony for close to two years. (For comparison, Mozart composed his 40th Symphony in six weeks.) Brahms's care is evident in the economy of his music—the first page of the symphony contains almost every motif that will be used in the first movement and allusions to those in later movements—and in his exceptional sense for balancing different instruments and their timbres, dynamics, and projections. Good conductors lead their orchestras to realize the breadth and passion of Brahms's vision; Mr. Bychkov, however, gave an unfocused and occasionally confusing performance that failed to live up to the majesty of Brahms's last symphony.

The first movement, allegro non troppo (not too quickly), was instead taken molto allegro (very quickly). Granted, the musical trends over the last century have slowed our performances (Leonard Bernstein's infamous comments before the Brahms D Minor concerto with Glenn Gould come to mind), but this interpretation rushed through the grand descending thirds that are so important to the first movement, and to the piece at large. The second theme had an overemphasis on the horns when the cellos should have taken center stage, and they were once again over-imposing during the coda. Whether Mr. Bychkov felt that the horns ought to be foregrounded or the horn section lacked clear direction from him, the music felt malproportioned and incoherent.

Nevertheless, there were fine, even excellent moments throughout. The opening, despite its overeager tempo, was gentle and flowing; by hushing the orchestra in certain transitional passages, a sense of tension, even menace, was delicately conjured; the woodwinds were perfectly poised—bright and soaring over the strings, but not at all sharp or shrill—during the transition to the recapitulation.

The second movement held the mistake which was by far the most inexcusable throughout the evening: either the horns misinterpreted Mr. Bychkov's gesture, or his gesture was ambiguous; in either case, the horns failed to enter all together on the same beat. After that hiccup, matters improved, though in the second movement's climax, the timpani, which should provide the unifying beat for the entire orchestra, instead pounded out its marching call so hard that other instruments were muffled. That this was the first performance of this program is no reason for the Philharmonic to be in disarray, nor for Mr. Bychkov to fail during crucial moments. Once again, it was during the less famous passages that the orchestra sounded its best: the woodwinds uncoiled their melody as if it were a delicate filigree, while the troublesome timpani gave a perfect drumroll in the movement's coda.

The third movement was, surprisingly, the best—surprising because it is less radical than the other three and thus more difficult to produce an exceptional interpretation. That is, it's easier to make a memorable soufflé—well-known, but unconventional—than a memorable grilled cheese. And yet, the pacing was perfect, the various sections of the orchestra were careful of each other's music, and it was all brought together by Mr. Bychkov, who seemed a tad less restrained and a little more able to enjoy the moment than before.

However, by the fourth movement, the magic had dissipated: the music was unsteady and lacked verve and heft. Both the opening and closing chorale was almost sped through, which meant that it lacked the darkness and tragedy which comprises the spirit of the finale. Moreover, the climactic 25th variation, with despairing violins and halting, fate-knocking-at-the-door brass—for a truly uplifting experience, listen to Mr. Bernstein with the Vienna Philharmonic—felt strangely toothless, and it failed even to stir. Once again, the movement was not without its merits—the fourth variation, with its deceptively simple rhythmic arpeggio, could move one to tears, while the 16th variation held a sense of pain previously unmatched all evening. But these moments of delight, passion, or ecstasy could not compensate for an overall incoherent and unbalanced evening. It was saddening to see the Philharmonic, and Mr. Bychkov, who has produced excellent recordings of works as challenging as Shostakovich's Seventh Symphony, this hapless.
