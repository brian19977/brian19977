Concert Reviews: Mitsuko Uchida and Richard Goode, 24 and 26 February 2023

One of the most challenging, most intense, and certainly most common piano recital programs is composed of the last three sonatas by Ludwig van Beethoven, opp. 109, 110, and 111. The bridge between the Classical and Romantic periods—the capstone of the New Testament of the keyboard—the so-called holy cows of piano literature: their status as civilizational artifact almost overwhelms their innate beauty, pathos and ecstasy.

Your correspondent was planning to hear the program three times—three times!—in New York in 2020, the quarter-millenial of Beethoven's birth: performed by Jonathan Biss, at the 92nd St. Y; by Steven Osborne, at Alice Tully Hall; and by Maurizio Pollini, at Carnegie Hall. (Mr. Pollini's recital was part of a series of all-Beethoven recitals by eminent pianists—besides Mr. Pollini: Emanuel Ax, Evgeny Kissin, Sir András Schiff, Yefim Bronfman, Igor Levit, and Mitsuko Uchida, to whom we shall return in a moment.)

But Beethoven's quarter-millenial was in 2020, and so none of those concerts took place.

But in the 2022-23 season, the program was to be performed at Carnegie Hall by Mitsuko Uchida—who, incidentally, would have played the Diabelli Variations, Beethoven's final significant work for the piano, for her contribution to the aforementioned Carnegie Hall series. As if that weren't enough, Richard Goode would play another all-Beethoven program, of bagatelles, the sonata op. 109 (as well), and—funnily enough!—the Diabelli Variations just two days later and two miles uptown (at the 92nd St. Y).

That is more than enough prologue; please forgive your correspondent.

It bears mention that both Dame Uchida and Mr. Goode are advanced in years: the former, in her mid-seventies; the latter, a few months shy of eighty. These are not easy programs; they are among the hardest that a pianist of any age might perform. So it is with the highest respect that your correspondent looks back positively and happily on both attendances. These éminences grises felt the weight of the challenge, but also undertook it with aplomb and finesse.

*    *    *

Dame Uchida has even performed this program before at Carnegie Hall, in 2009 (not too long ago, given her six-decade career). Though your correspondent did not attend that performance, his anticipation, stoked by her Beethoven sonata recordings, had him fit to burst while waiting for the concert to begin; the night's performances, however, were richer, more delightful versions of her already-excellent interpretations.

The first sonata—in E Major, op. 109—is the most benevolent of the trio. Its tight construction—the first movement's primary theme is stated in eight seconds, and then moves onto the secondary theme; most of the material for the entire piece originates within the first minute of the piece; there is consensus but not agreement that the piece is even three movements—augments its wandering, contented idiom; listening to the piece feels like visiting the same place over many years, same but different.

Dame Uchida concentrated her efforts on highlighting this meandering quality. Her genteel, flowing opening evoked some pastoral expanse, dotted with sheep and rabbits; the second movement had an appropriately tumultuous quality, evoking cracks of thunder interrupting the quiet reverie. The third movement felt particularly special: the music opens, quietly choral, before becoming at turns amiable, sarcastic, and didactic; by its end, it transfigures into a soaring, shuddering, archangel of a finale. Dame Uchida's chorale was celestial; she gamely negotiated the unceasing transformations to reach the ecstatic finale. Her performance shocked with its vitality and delirium; it yearned for and achieved flight.

Dame Uchida made a curious request, conveyed in a small statement printed in the program: that applause be withheld between the first and second sonatas of the program. This is not an intuitive request; the sonatas don't particularly flow into each other, nor are they close harmonic siblings. Whether because people forgot, or were overwhelmed by her performance, or simply missed the announcement (your correspondent didn't even open his program until after the performance), applause was generally given. Dame Uchida cast a sidelong glance at her audience before proceeding.

Dame Uchida's second sonata was even better than her first. The sonata in A-flat Major, op. 110, is often described as the most humane piece among Beethoven's late piano works: it is the most straightforwardly lyrical, the funniest, the most tragic, and the most uplifting.

Once again, Dame Uchida accentuated the work's inner character, to great effect. The sweetly lilting opening melody led to crystalline arpeggios shooting four and five octaves up and down the keyboard; the second movement's middle section was jaunty and brisk; the angular, almost dry fugue of the last movement turned dour and heavy as the mood fit. That fugue is interrupted by a chord described by Sir András Schiff as "pure tragedy;" the transition from the fugue into the grieving arioso section, marked "exhausted" and "weeping" in the score, was the best of any interpretation your correspondent has ever heard. The finale, a hymnal chorale, felt all the more uplifting for the depths of mourning reached in the arioso.

The majesty of these two sonatas is to some degree predicated on drawing sufficient contrast between their quietude and climaxes; Dame Uchida's biggest shortcomings were in underpowered fortes and insufficient drama. Her arpeggios—so perfectly sparkling in the second sonata—were limpid and weak in the first; her chords throughout failed to fill the cavernous hall. The finale of the second sonata, marked "again reviving" and "little by little with renewed vigor," felt less like a glorious resurrection than a figure once great slowly rising again.

After the intermission came the sonata in C Minor, op. 111, a behemoth of two contrasting movements: "minor/major, fast/slow, harmonic agitation/harmonic stasis, angular/melodic, propulsive/static, terrestrial/divine," for an incomplete list from one commentator. Its position as Beethoven's final statement in the genre which captivated him more than any other (Beethoven wrote 32 piano sonatas, double the second-most populated genre, his 16 string quartets) may belie, rather than augment, its being not only a bafflingly beautiful work but possibly the most bewildering single piece ever written for the piano.

Dame Uchida's performance was shakiest during this (extremely difficult, impossible to perfect) sonata: she had multiple memory slips; she fumbled her scales into mucky, uneven chunks; the mystical filigree, evoking fog on the Styx, was just foggy rather than nervously uneasy. But her passion was still sufficiently felt; the second movement was almost perfect. A theme and variations based on rhythmic, rather than melodic or harmonic mutation, the movement opens with a glacial lullaby whose notes are subdivided to make the same melody sound first like a ballad, and then a playground chant, and then a proto-blues cacophony, and then a kaleidoscopic repeat of the theme before trills of piercing, shimmering birdsong. The ballad was yearning, hopeful; the cacophony, joyful and electric. The repeat of the theme late in the piece was warmly embracing; its deep sense of nostos made for possibly the most moving minute of a piano recital your correspondent has ever heard in his life.

The audience's gratitude poured forth in overwhelming swells; New Yorkers are always appreciative of their musicians, but this sold-out hall gave the great Dame five standing ovations. She raised her arms—you all are too kind—and bowed deeply at her waist. The final sonata can only lead into nothingness, and Dame Uchida wisely and understandably declined to give an encore. It was the first time your correspondent had seen the lights come up before, rather than after, the applause concluded at Carnegie Hall: no more music was needed.

*    *    *

Two days later ...

The 92nd St. Y billed the concert as a "completion" of the complete Beethoven sonata cycle Mr. Goode had performed there in the late 1980s; temporal gap aside, the label is not inappropriate, insofar as the Diabelli Variations are not only in musical and expressive communion with the late sonatas, but also contain a neat numeric Easter egg: there are 32 sonatas, and the set has 33 variations.

The last time your correspondent heard the Diabelli Variations—with Paul Lewis at the same venue—he was sorely disappointed with the performance; not only was the first half of this program a more fitting setup for the monumental variations, the performance itself was far more to your correspondent's liking: Mr. Goode's subtlety and wit shone brilliantly throughout this hour-long masterpiece.

Mr. Goode's selection of bagatelles from the set, op. 119, began abruptly, and felt somewhat prosaic; he seemed more to be warming up both himself and the audience rather than making a worthwhile statement of late-Beethoven miniatures. The audience was still shuffling about, trying to find their seats, and turning off phones and taking off coats well into the third or fourth out of seven bagatelles.

With the audience properly settled in after the perhaps ten-minute introduction (and the hall doors were opened to allow in audience members before the next piece), the sonata, op. 109, began again. Your correspondent first noticed that Mr. Goode's op. 109 felt more angular, more powerful than Dame Uchida's by some distance: the arpeggiated passages in the first movement rang throughout the hall; the chords of the tumultuous second movement, much more overwhelming. Mr. Goode's overall slower tempi suited the piece throughout as well: if an interpretation favors fervor and drive, faster tempi suit better; if the music's details and subtleties are to be displayed; slower tempi would be prudent. Mr. Goode's finale was less transfigurative, less Pentecostal than Dame Uchida's, but he made up for it with a balanced, introspective performance.

The Diabelli Variations—"33 Variations on a Waltz by Anton Diabelli" is its full title—is, in the words of the eminent pianist Hans von Bülow, "a microcosm of Beethoven's art."

(If we are speaking of Beethoven, von Bülow may be his greatest acolyte: he is not only the first pianist known to have played all 32 sonatas in a concert series, but he was also known for giving such mammoth recitals as Beethoven's complete late sonatas—no. 28, op. 101, and no. 29, op. 106, the famous Hammerklavier, nearly fifty minutes just on its own, in addition to the opp. 109, 110, and 111. Even your correspondent would have balked at attending such a concert.)

The hour-long set contains bombastic marches; cutesy parodies; sphinxlike adagios; extended, grieving recitatives; and a monster of a fugue somehow simultaneously self-deprecating and hyperarticulate.

Mr. Goode's tema (the original waltz by Diabelli) was jovial, innocent without being immature. He smoothly transitioned to the first variation, and the variations were off. Mr. Goode observed every repeat, but did not embellish or augment the music in any noticeable way—studiousness without creativity, in your correspondent's opinion. But his playing was of such excellence that he hardly minded Mr. Goode's musical long-windedness.

Each of the Diabelli Variations has its own personality; Mr. Goode led them in delightful procession. The first variation, a march, was taken slower than usual, adding to its stateliness; the seventh variation, with a minimum of pedal, pealed joyfully its C Major bells. The music was muddled at times—a variation of relatively minor note, such as the eighth, felt trodden over for the sake of completion—while lacking precision and focus in more dexterous variations like the tenth and twenty-seventh. But these were rare blips in a relatively straightforwadly enjoyable first forty minutes: the thirteenth, a minimalist variation of rhetorical chords and long silences, flipped perfectly between brashness and whispers; gossamer-thin pianissimo filigree was woven through the fourteenth; the energetic sixteenth and seventeenth never slowed an inch through pinballing broken octaves and shuddering trills.

In the last third, however, Mr. Goode showed himself a cut above: being aged just shy of eighty, he proved, is not only not a liability, but an asset of incalculable value when it comes to expressing the passion of late Beethoven most completely. The twenty-ninth variation, a slow lamenting chant, was taken slower than your correspondent has ever heard it, but it only functioned to make the grief more keenly felt. The thirty-first variation, an extended elegy with rich chromatic color, unspooled its long melodic lines smoothly, elegantly, but never dryly. And the thirty-second variation—the fugue which had eventually defeated your correspondent—well, Mr. Goode dispatched with panache and wit, again taking a slower tempo (this is the only variation about which your correspondent is convinced that Mr. Goode changed his interpretation due to technical limitations, though he may just be projecting) which served to clarify articulations and inner voices often neglected in faster recordings.

The twenty-ninth through thirty-second variations, one should point out, are the only variations not in C Major: the first three of them are in C Minor, while the fourth is in E-flat Major, the relative major of C Minor. Which is to say, the most contemplative, most reverential, most purely expressive music in the entire set is also harmonically adrift, sometimes veering on atonal. So, when the final, thirty-third variation returns to C Major, its sense of homecoming is not unlike that of the finale of the op. 111: almost unbearably beautiful, felt more in the heart (and tear ducts) than the brain. A gentle minuet concludes the journey through the musical cosmos, and Mr. Goode gave it all he had: perfect staccatos, legatos that flowed like oil, twinkling figuration high in the keyboard that somehow evoked the mystical state right before one falls asleep: perfectly at peace, ready for whatever may come next. A brief C Major chord marked the end; the audience cheered and cheered. Mr. Goode, too, received four or five standing ovations; he, too, wisely declined to give an encore.

*    *    *

A number of pianists, excellent in their youth or middle age, came to give effortful, mediocre, cringeworthy performances late in life. Maurizio Pollini gave an all-Chopin recital so awful—scales performed fitfully, with zero clarity; climactic octaves barely above mezzo-forte; overpedaling in a sad attempt to muddle through challenging sections—that your correspondent turned away from his future recitals until he saw that Mr. Pollini would perform the last three sonatas. Radu Lupu's last seasons were marred with memory slips, fumbled passagework, and, in concerto performances, zero coordination between him and the orchestra. Daniel Barenboim still pretends to be a conductor-pianist when he is really a conductor who can play the piano.

But Dame Uchida and Mr. Goode are not just good for their age—they are good, they are excellent, they very much are still on top of it. The sold-out halls showed that people still love Beethoven; the sustained applause showed that people still love them. As does your correspondent. May they grace our stages for years to come; may their august, autumnal period never end.
