Concert Dispatch: Yefim Bronfman and Rafael Payare with the Montréal Symphony Orchestra (OSM), 8 March 2023

A Carnegie Hall veteran and a new orchestra director teamed up for an exciting, excellent concert of Chang, Bartók, and Mahler. The evening opened with Chang's Precipice, meant to evoke everything from COVID to racial injustice to climate danger. It was followed by the Hungarian master's Piano Concerto no. 2, one of the most finger-busting works in the repertoire, performed by Yefim Bronfman, a pianist who may inspire respect more than raving but whose technique and musicality are unimpeachable. The second half of the concert was dedicated to Mahler's Symphony no. 5, one of his most recognizable—from its fate-motif opening to its glorious Adagietto—whose popularity has only grown thanks to its prominence in the recently-released Tár. Rafael Payare led his eager, nimble ensemble through this hefty evening with verve and elegance. Though not every crucial moment was together, the musicians put together a splendid performance that had its audience smiling on the way out; your correspondent looks forward to the group's future visits.

Any piece of music meant to capture the world is bound either to fall short or overwhelm; Ms. Chang's Precipice is to be lauded for its vision and present-ness but does not say much about anything. Hopefully her other compositions are more likely to succeed in their stated goals.

With the Bartók concerto, the OSM had abundant opportunity to show off its brass and winds, and they did not pass it up. The first movement, scored without any strings, is an exercise in balance—between jagged chords from the piano, delicate melodies from the winds, and grand blasts from the brass; Payare coaxed forward the easily-overwhelmed flutes and held the trombones in check, making a lovely, glossy sheen to undergird the galloping piano solo. When the strings came in during the second movement, a warm, earthen mood pervaded; the piano became a lyric instrument, singing in rich, dark-yellow tones the songs of Bartók's homeland. The third movement was a riot from start to finish; in the cadenza before the finale, Bronfman let it all loose—from the faux-hesitant start to the slam-bang (in the best sense) runs which closed out the piece. Well-earned cheers and ovations convinced Mr. Bronfman to give an encore of Chopin's Nocturne in D-flat Major; it too was rich, golden—just gorgeous.

Mahler's Fifth has a peculiar structure: five movements and three parts. The first and second, both funeral marches, comprise part one; the third, a horn concerto, is part two; the aforementioned Adagietto and an exuberant finale make part the last. A successful performance must not only be enjoyable moment-to-moment but pull together this seventy-minute, multipartite beast into a coherent whole. Mr. Payare did not quite get full marks on this challenging task, but his interpretation did yield novel insights and enjoyments.

For example, the famous opening trumpet call was performed ad libitum—that is, Mr. Payare declined to conduct it, the only conductor your correspondent knows of who decided thus. It was an excellent choice, one which allowed the trumpet to hark rather than to announce; it felt more solemn, more trauermarsch. And at the end of the movement, the ensemble was slowed way down; the last chord was taken as quietly as possible—also something your correspondent has never heard, and something immediately and totally convincing.

The muscular second movement led into the third, where the OSM's marvelous principal horn stole the show. Here, too, Mr. Payare gave the soloist a free hand, letting her take her breaths and then expel them in arcing lines of passion and warmth. In the Adagietto—taken faster than many recent interpretations, and better off for it—pianissimo plucking from the basses grounded the remaining strings' miraculous performance of this love song. Mr. Payare reined in the ensemble as it swelled and let it go as it reached the musical climax, resulting in a tight buildup whose release was all the more explosive for it.

The only true faults of the evening were in the last movement. The musicians, weary from a long concert; Mr. Payare, still unsure of a dense, contrapuntal finale; the audience, not yet awoken from the glazed happiness of the Adagietto—all factors amalgamated in a haphazard, unconvincing finale. In the last minute or so, though, with brass fanfares and soaring strings, both the musicians and the audience collected themselves for the symphony's celestial end—perhaps the most humane, and straightforwardly joyful, of all of Mahler's symphonies. The ovation went on and on; Mr. Payare introduced each of his sections in turn. Each and every one deserved its cheer.
