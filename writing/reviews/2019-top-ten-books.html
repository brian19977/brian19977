Top 10 Books of 2019

10. The Underground Railroad by Colson Whitehead.

This book received delighting reviews from every newspaper with a literature section when it was published, and they were not wrong: Whitehead maneuvers what could be a superficial and theatrical twist on the historical escape route of slaves dexterously and grippingly. His characters step from the page, whether they describe the loss of a one-yard-square plot of land to their fellow slaves or their horror as they watch less-fortunate escapees lynched in the public square before aloof, almost-bored townspeople. Besides its gimmick (and a well-executed one, as stated), however, it has little to distinguish it from other slave narratives, well-written as many of them are.

9. Immortaility by Milan Kundera.

Kundera's novels always leave your correspondent slightly dizzy: in their plot, in their philosophy, in their questions, they tend to orbit some indeterminate point until they hit you backside the head and leave you, flattened, questioning all that you've known. Featuring a fascinating ensemble of characters, from a tire-slashing professor to Johann Wolfgang von Goethe to the author himself, the book compels us to face deep-seated prejudices, hopes, and beliefs and ask questions so bafflingly deep they seem parodical: "Who am I?" "What does it mean to live?" "What is legacy?" For mordant wit, piercing intelligence, and a langorous plot, look no further.

8. Tender is the Night by F. Scott Fitzgerald.

Five years after The Great Gatsby, readers awaited the release of the hard-writing, harder-partying Fitzgerald's new book, to respond with dismay. Sure, the mental illness of Nicole and alcoholism of Dick reflect the turbulence underlying their glamorous lifestyle, but the greatest writers, when seemingly at the zenith of their creative powers, are, rather, often channeling their powers of observation and articulation. The winding, brooding, inky blackness of a novel is not meant to cheer one up, but then neither is The Odyssey. Rarely has the final line of a novel landed so punishingly.

7. In Cold Blood by Truman Capote.

Have you ever read a story in The New Yorker and wished that it went on for a bit longer? Say, 330 pages longer? Capote's true crime nonfiction novel, sensational when first released, retains its capacity to chill today. It expends enormous text explicating the backgrounds, characters, and thought processes of the two murderers, and while this does not necessarily make for the most interesting reading, the scenes of their back-to-back hanging, as well as their process by which they were convicted and sentenced—just by all appearance, and yet entirely backward considering modern psychology—finally compelled your correspondent to come out as against the death penalty.

6. On Chesil Beach by Ian McEwan and The Sense of an Ending by Julian Barnes.

These two slender ( < 200 pages) novels by British authors follow superficially different but essentially similar scenarios for the modern conscience to contemplate: how do we coexist with people of different temperaments, upbringings, and worldviews? McEwan's novel follows, with flashbacks, the wedding night of a couple whose sexual opinions are diametrically opposed; Barnes conjures an elderly man tracking down the diary of a deceased friend, bumping into ghosts of his past along the way. Both seem to say that we are hopeless in changing the ways of others, but that there is hope for reconciliation—or, at least, understanding. These are both perfect novels to be read in a single day—somewhere autumn leaves are blowing or spring rains are pattering.

5. Rules of Civility by Amor Towles.

The first novel of an investment-banker-turned-writer, the rise of two girls seeking fame and fortune in 1930s New York is bound to charm your correspondent. But this one has an especial place in the pantheon of New York novels—no mean feat, considering that it is packed to capacity already by such eminences as Edith Wharton, Tom Wolfe, and Garth Risk Hallberg and his modern beast City on Fire (which would have made last year's list had your correspondent thought to compose one)—because it takes loneliness, indignity, and mundanity—the bread and butter of a New York lifestyle—and transfigures them into the vitality and exuberance we so love in our city. Though more modest in ambition than its successor, it resonates with the spirit of the city at a deeper frequency.

4. Purity by Jonathan Franzen.

Given that Franzen is your correspondent's favorite author, it may be unfair to other authors to place him on this list—"Come on, we know you like him, give others a chance for once"—but de gustibus non disputandum est, and so here is another Franzen appraisal. Authors must earn their stars with every new book, and, while many felt that Purity was a significant disappointment after Freedom, your author felt that it was a boundary-breaking moment of growth for the sexagenarian author. Up until now, Franzen concerned himself with relationships—three of his previous four novels had centered on a single family's dynamic, while the other examined a single couple's. But in Purity, Franzen examined lovers, friends, enemies, a mother and daughter, and hookup partners—in short, more heterodox than ever fleshed out before. It, more than any other of his oeuvre, is most perennially present, rather than an encasement of the times during which it was wrought—important as that undeniably is. Slow first 50 pages? Get over it—it's worth it.

3. The Idiot by Fyodor Dostoevsky.

Your correspondent first read this novel in the summer entering tenth grade—no wonder, then, that he remembered little of the plot or themes at the start of his second reading, many years later. The years had only deepened his sense of the tragic, though, and so Prince Myshkin's hapless goings-on with St. Petersburg's ruthless, heartless nobility struck him as unbearably sad, as opposed to merely unpleasant. Dostoevsky's masterpieces—in your correspondent's opinion, more so than Crime and Punishment, almost certainly his better-known work—are hard to distil into one hundred fifty words, let alone a book of their own right, but this will have to do.

2. Hamlet by William Shakespeare.

Ditto the above: the ease of a work's distil-ability is in inverse proportion to its mastery. This being your correspondent's first reading, he was more focused on matters of plot than of theme or language, but all came together in a literally breathtaking sweep. It's perhaps the first work of Shakespeare that made your correspondent viscerally feel how amazing of a writer Shakespeare was.

1. A Little Life by Hanya Yanagihara.

The book, even before one opens to its first page, is unlike most others: the cover depicts a man in intense pain or sorrow, and it lands with a thud (it totals over 800 pages). But the physical weight of the book becomes a feather in comparison to its emotional barrage, which might be compared to a bullet train to the gut. Speaking of guts: if Chuck Palahnuik's "Guts" could reduce a room to mass exodus and bouts of fainting, A Little Life can reduce even the most temperamentally reticent individuals (read: yours truly) to weeping. Yanagihara's excellence lies in making her readers empathize and invest with her characters, and then ruining our souls by ruining their lives. So great is the pathos it depicts that your correspondent started reading about the Yemeni civil war as a reprieve from the novel. Wrong though it may be to read about real suffering as a salve to imagined suffering, one cannot help but fall for Yanagihara's trap: she's just that good. Though the book could have used a slightly more judicious editor, for length as well as plot, its indulgences can be forgiven: any missteps pale in comparison to the fact that this novel is the only work of art—music, painting, statuary, literature—that has ever made your correspondent cry.

Bonus <> a book not worth reading: American Pastoral by Philip Roth.

There are exquisite moments, and there are interminable quarters of an hour. Philip Roth is an American icon, and yet no verb better captures your correspondent's reading experience than "trudging." Pulitzer be damned, this one's going to the "sell back to the Strand" box.
